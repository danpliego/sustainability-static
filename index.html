Wildlife Corridors

Wildlife corridors are pathways in water, land, and air in which birds, mammals, invertebrates, reptiles, and insects migrate, feed, drink, and move to and from larger areas of habitat.

Human development, including housing, roads, and freeways, has chopped habitat into fragments too small to sustain biodiversity, and introduced dangerous, often impassable boundaries that block migration and threaten wildlife populations.

With time, this practice grew entrenched, and we've been mass producing and distributing exotic plants ever since. As foreign plants spread, invasive species escaped and colonized ecosystems across the country.

How do I decide what plants are best for my yard?

Let’s review the three main ways we distinguish environmentally-friendly plantings

Climate Adapted

Climate adapted plants are good for water conservation (equivalent to appropriate natives), but not nearly as good for habitat. Some climate adapted plants are great habitat for generalist species, but the majority of local fauna relies on natives for habitat.

Native

Native plants have the best habitat value, because they have adapted to the local environment. They provide the basis of life for local birds and bugs. Side note: Keystones are the most productive natives - think Oak trees!

Pollinator

A pollinator plant could be either native or climate adapted, but natives are more productive when it come to supporting habitats. However, some non-natives have proven to help support pollinators communities.

How can you help make a difference?

Plant Native & Keystones

Provide more food, water, and shelter for birds, bees, and other critical critters. All in the name of biodiversity.

Plant Trees

Cut back on air pollution, urban heat islands, and home energy use.

Permeable Paving & Planting Beds

Slow water down and soak it back into the ground before it can overwhelm city drains. This cuts down on flooding and water pollution
